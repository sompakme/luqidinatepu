Sunflower symbolism: What is this flower supposed to represent?

Sunflower symbolism is one of the most interesting concepts today. From the flower's colour meanings to stories and myths that revolve around the bloom, people associate it with a lot of good tidings, warmth and luck.

But what is the authentic sunflower meaning, if there is any? Well, the meaning has its origin in many places, with different parts of the world associating it with different things.

Common meaning of sunflower

As already stated,the meaning of sunflowers differ and vary depending on the place. Even so, many cultures around the world share certain meanings and attributes of the bloom based on its psychical attributes.

Some of the common meanings include:

  • Long life. This is mainly because most varieties stand in full bloom for months on end during the hottest days of summer despite the harsh weather.
  • Feelings of adoration, admiration, and platonic love towards a person, including family members and friends.
  • Loyalty and strong bonds between two people, as represented by the strong and upright stem.
  • Seeking out positivity and strength, which is indicated by the bloom that turns to face the sun.
  • Nourishing yourself and others, as the bloom is known to produce an abundance of edible seeds
  • Brightening your mood, through the vibrancy of the yellow or orange petals
  • It may also refer to good luck and lasting happiness, especially according to the Chinese culture

History and origin of sunflower

The above descriptions give a general meaning of the sunflower. However, what does sunflower mean etymologically?

There are different versions that describe the origin of the plant. One of the most popular is the Greek version, but it is not the only one since an American version exist.

The Greek story of the sunflower

According to the Greek, there existed a Greek water nymph by the name Clytie. Clytie fell in love with the Sun God named Apollo.

She always looked at him with the hope that he would be interested in her. Unfortunately, Apollo was interested in someone else, hence did not look in Clytie's way.

It took Clytie a long while to realize that Apollo had no interest in her. She became depressed as a result of the one-sided love.

She ended up not eating nor drinking anything as she just stared at Apollo. After some time, other Greek gods felt sorry for her and decided to transform her into a beautiful sunflower instead of leaving her the way she was.

The American story of the sunflower

Apart from the Greek story, an American story about the plant's beginning also exists. It is said that the plant is indigenous to America.

According to this story, in the 16th century, Incan priestesses in Peru worshipped a giant variety of the plant, which was a symbol of the Incan sun god.

The Incan priestesses would dress in clothes and ornaments with large flower shapes. The ornaments and decorations were purely made from gold.

Existing images show that the designs were discovered in Peruvian temples. This was a major part of their religious beliefs back then.

Native Americans planted it for food and other uses. It is also believed that it was a very important Native American crop that was also worshipped by the community.

Apart from being a source of food for them, the bloom was also used as a symbol of strength and endurance. In fact, the plant was such an important part of their life such that they always put its seeds on top of graves.
